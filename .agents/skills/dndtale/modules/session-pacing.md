# Session Pacing & Dramatic Structure

Every session should, when possible, follow its own dramatic arc within the larger campaign. Pacing is the difference between a session that feels like a story and one that feels like a to-do list.

---

## The Session Arc

### 1. Cold Open (5–10 minutes)

Start in the middle of something. Recap through action, not summary.

**Do:** "Last we left off, you were knee-deep in swamp water with something *large* moving beneath the surface."

**Don't:** "Last session you traveled to the swamp and talked to some NPCs."

The cold open should re-engage the players immediately. Drop them into tension, sensation, or a decision. Even a quiet opening can have urgency — "You wake to the smell of smoke" is a cold open.

### 2. Rising Action (60–90 minutes)

The main content of the session — exploration, investigation, social encounters, travel, smaller combats. This is where the story builds.

**Key principles:**
- Build tension gradually — each scene should raise the stakes or deepen the mystery slightly more than the last
- Alternate between high-energy and low-energy scenes to prevent fatigue
- Plant seeds for the session's climax — foreshadow, build dread, create anticipation
- Let players drive the pace when possible — follow their curiosity

### 3. Climax (30–60 minutes)

The session's dramatic peak — a major combat, a critical revelation, a gut-punch NPC moment, or a decision that can't be undone.

**The climax should feel earned.** Everything in the rising action was building to this. The information they gathered, the allies they made (or lost), the terrain they scouted — it all matters here.

Not every climax is combat. A climax can be:
- The moment they realize the ally was the traitor
- A moral choice with no good options
- A desperate negotiation with the clock running out
- The reveal that changes everything they thought they knew

### 4. Falling Action (15–30 minutes)

Process the climax. Let players react, plan, loot, argue, grieve, celebrate, and breathe.

This is where character moments happen. The quiet conversation after the battle. The argument about what to do with the prisoner. The moment someone says "My character walks away from the group and sits alone." **Don't rush this.** These are the scenes players remember as much as the fights.

### 5. Cliffhanger / Hook (5 minutes)

End with a question that makes them count the days until next session.

- A distant horn sounds from the direction they're heading
- A letter arrives bearing a seal they recognize
- The NPC they trusted says something that doesn't add up
- The villain's plan clicks into focus — and it's worse than they thought

**The best cliffhangers are promises.** They promise the players that next session will answer a question they desperately want answered.

## Pacing Tools

### The Ticking Clock

Add a deadline to raise urgency. The ritual completes at midnight. The dam breaks in three days. The assassination happens at the feast. The poison kills in six hours.

Ticking clocks transform exploration from leisurely browsing into agonizing triage. When time is finite, every choice about where to go and what to investigate becomes meaningful.

**Use when:** the session feels low-stakes or aimless, or when you want to force hard choices about priorities.

### The Breather Scene

After intense sequences, give players a campfire conversation, a tavern night, a shopping montage, or a quiet walk through the city. Let characters *be* characters.

Breather scenes serve three purposes:
1. **Emotional processing** — players need time to react to what just happened
2. **Character development** — inter-party relationships deepen in downtime
3. **Contrast** — the next intense scene hits harder because of the calm before it

**Use when:** you've just run an intense combat, a major revelation, or a high-stakes social encounter.

### The Dramatic Cut

End a scene at its peak moment and cut to a different location, character, or timeline.

"The arrow flies toward your face — and we cut to what's happening in the throne room."

Dramatic cuts build suspense, allow parallel storytelling, and keep energy high across multiple storylines. They work especially well in campaigns with split-party moments or competing timelines.

**Use when:** you want to build suspense, switch between split groups, or prevent a scene from losing momentum by running too long.

### The Information Drip

Don't dump exposition. Reveal story information through NPC conversations, environmental details, recovered documents, overheard arguments, and physical evidence. Let players assemble the picture.

The information drip respects player intelligence. Instead of "the villain's plan is X," give them three puzzle pieces across three scenes and let them put it together themselves. The moment of realization — when a player says "Wait... does that mean..." — is one of the most satisfying moments in tabletop gaming.

**Use when:** always. Exposition dumps are almost never the right choice.

## Pacing Across Multiple Sessions

### Campaign-Level Arc

The session arc (Cold Open → Climax → Cliffhanger) nests inside the campaign arc:

- **Act 1 (Sessions 1–3):** Establish the world, introduce the conflict, make the players care
- **Act 2 (Sessions 4–N):** Escalate complications, deepen relationships, raise stakes, present dilemmas
- **Act 3 (Final sessions):** Converge storylines, resolve major tensions, deliver the climax

### Avoiding the Mid-Campaign Sag

The middle of a campaign is where pacing most often fails. Combat this with:
- **Tentpole encounters** — pre-planned dramatic peaks that punctuate the middle sessions
- **Escalation** — each session should feel like the situation is getting more urgent, complex, or personal
- **Character-driven sessions** — shift focus to PC backstories and inter-party dynamics when the main plot needs breathing room
- **Subplots** — weave secondary storylines that pay off during the middle act
