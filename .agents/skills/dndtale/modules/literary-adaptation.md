# Literary Adaptation Framework

When adapting novels, films, mythology, TV series, or other source material into D&D campaign content, follow this structured process. The goal is to preserve what makes the source compelling while transforming it into something that lives and breathes through player agency.

---

## The Adaptation Framework

### Phase 1: Core Extraction

Before designing anything, analyze the source material deeply:

**Identify the thematic spine** — what is this story *actually about* beneath the plot? The Dark Tower isn't about reaching a tower; it's about obsession, ka, and the cost of single-minded purpose. Lord of the Rings isn't about destroying a ring; it's about ordinary courage in the face of overwhelming evil.

**Map the emotional arc** — what should players *feel* at each stage? Chart the emotional journey: Wonder → Unease → Dread → Resolve → Sacrifice → Catharsis. This arc is your campaign's skeleton even when the plot changes.

**Catalog iconic moments** — scenes that define the source material and must be preserved in some form. These become your campaign's tentpole encounters. The Battle of Helm's Deep. The Red Wedding. The reveal in the cave on Dagobah. Players should feel the *weight* of these moments even in translated form.

**Identify player-agency friction points** — where does the source material's predetermined plot conflict with the need for player choice? A novel's protagonist makes specific decisions. Your players won't make those same decisions. These friction points are your redesign priorities.

### Phase 2: Mechanical Translation

Convert narrative elements into playable D&D content:

**Convert narrative themes into playable moral dilemmas** with no clean answers. "Power corrupts" becomes a magic item that grows stronger with morally questionable uses. "Redemption is possible" becomes a villain who genuinely wants to change — but can the party trust them?

**Translate literary locations to game-setting equivalents** that provide player familiarity. Map fictional geography to Exandria, Forgotten Realms, or homebrew settings. The source's haunted castle maps onto Ravenloft's aesthetic. The source's political court maps onto Emon or Waterdeep.

**Redesign key characters as NPCs with independent agency** — they have goals, resources, and plans that exist whether the players engage with them or not. The novel's protagonist becomes an NPC ally (or rival) pursuing their own version of the quest.

**Transform set-piece scenes into encounters with multiple resolution paths** — combat, social, stealth, arcane, environmental. The source's inevitable showdown becomes a situation with three ways in, two ways out, and a wild card the players might invent.

### Phase 3: Integration & Departure

Decide where to honor and where to diverge:

**Faithfully echo** the source where emotional beats, thematic resonance, and iconic imagery define the experience. The *feeling* of a moment matters more than its exact plot mechanics.

**Deliberately diverge** where player agency demands it, where subverting expectations rewards players who know the source, or where mechanical depth enriches the experience beyond what passive storytelling allows.

**Build recognition moments** for players familiar with the source — easter eggs, thematic callbacks, inverted expectations — without making knowledge of the source required. A player who recognizes the reference should feel clever, not advantaged.

## Research Methodology

### For Literary Adaptations

- Perform chapter-by-chapter or act-by-act analysis identifying: central conflict, character motivations, setting details, tonal shifts, and thematic throughlines
- Create a **theme map** linking source themes to D&D mechanical expressions (e.g., "corruption" → gradual stat penalties, dark gift temptations, faction reputation shifts)
- Identify the source's **narrative structure** (hero's journey, mystery box, parallel timelines, picaresque) and map it to a compatible campaign type (linear, sandbox, event-based, setting-based)

### For Setting Research (Exandria, Forgotten Realms, etc.)

- Cross-reference official sourcebooks, wikis, and actual-play content for lore accuracy
- Identify existing factions, NPCs, and locations that can serve as anchors or analogs
- Note lore gaps or ambiguities that create creative freedom for original content
- Track the **political landscape** — who has power, who wants it, and what happens if the players tip the balance

### For Mechanical Research

- Verify stat blocks, spell interactions, and encounter balance against official rules and community best practices
- Research homebrew solutions when official content doesn't support the narrative need
- Consult encounter-building guidelines (CR calculations, action economy, terrain) but treat them as starting points, not gospels
- Study published adventures (Curse of Strahd, Rime of the Frostmaiden, etc.) for structural inspiration

## Novel/Series Adaptation Protocol

When adapting a specific novel or series, follow these four steps:

### Step 1: Read and Map

Create a chapter-by-chapter breakdown identifying: plot events, character introductions, setting descriptions, thematic developments, and tonal shifts.

Tag each element as:
- **ESSENTIAL** — must appear in some form (thematic core, iconic moments, structural foundations)
- **ADAPTABLE** — can be modified significantly (specific plot mechanics, secondary characters, exact sequences)
- **ATMOSPHERIC** — provides flavor but isn't structurally required (world details, minor scenes, background color)

### Step 2: Identify the Emotional Skeleton

Strip the plot to its emotional beats:
- "Character confronts the thing they fear most."
- "The trusted ally is revealed to be compromised."
- "Everything the heroes fought for is rendered meaningless — until they find a new reason to fight."

These beats are your campaign's structural foundations. The *how* changes; the *what it feels like* stays.

### Step 3: Build Player Entry Points

How do the PCs enter this story? They can't be the protagonist of the novel — they need to be *their own* protagonists in a world shaped by the novel's events.

**Options:**
- The PCs are **parallel characters** in the same world, pursuing related goals from a different angle
- The PCs are the novel's characters **reimagined with player agency** — same roles, different choices
- The PCs exist in a world **after the novel's events** — dealing with consequences and echoes
- The PCs are **originals in an adapted setting** — the world is the source, the story is new

### Step 4: Design the Departure Points

Identify 3–5 moments where player choice can fork the story away from the source material.

For each departure point:
- Design at least **two alternative paths** that maintain thematic integrity while creating genuine divergence
- Ensure both paths have meaningful consequences
- Plan how divergent paths might reconverge at later tentpole moments (or not)

These departure points are what make it a *campaign* instead of a *retelling*.

## Handling Players Who Know the Source

**Subvert expectations deliberately.** If they know the betrayal is coming, make the betrayer sympathetic first — or make a *different* character betray them. Use their knowledge as a tool for dramatic irony.

**Reward knowledge without requiring it.** A player who recognizes a reference should feel clever, not advantaged. Easter eggs and callbacks are gifts, not prerequisites.

**Let them be wrong.** If they think they know what happens next because they read the book, let the campaign surprise them. That surprise is a gift — the moment they realize this story is *theirs*, not a retelling.
